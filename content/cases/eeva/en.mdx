## Context

EEVA Studios is an emerging fashion brand with a very defined aesthetic direction: futuristic, dark, with visual references ranging from Blade Runner to cyberpunk culture. When we came to the project, they had brand identity in an early state — a logo, a palette, an attitude — but no digital presence.

The challenge wasn't technical. It was about translation: taking a visual universe that worked conceptually and turning it into an ecommerce experience that a real person could use to buy clothes.

A strong aesthetic can work against conversion if it isn't well anchored in structure. Most fashion ecommerce with visual ambitions fail at the same point: they prioritize impression over product clarity. The goal was for EEVA to avoid that trap.

## The problem

The brand needed to go from an initial identity to a complete digital experience that could do two things simultaneously: communicate a very specific visual universe and enable online purchasing clearly, without friction, and without the aesthetics competing with usability.

The underlying tension was real: ecommerce that looks "interesting" tends to be hard to use, and ecommerce that's easy to use tends to look generic. The client wanted both. That was the design problem.

The starting point complicated the process: no established visual system, no definitive product content, no reference for how the clothing would be photographed. Building structure on those open variables required making decisions that had to work across multiple future scenarios, not just for the brand's current state.

## Approach

Two principles guided the design.

**Aesthetics is function, not decoration.** In fashion, the visual direction of the ecommerce is part of the product being sold. If the experience doesn't convey the brand's world, the user doesn't understand what they're buying or why they'd want it. Visual design wasn't the layer on top of UX: it was UX.

**Structure has to be invisible.** The stronger the visual personality, the more important it is that navigation requires no effort. The flows for product discovery, cart, and checkout had to be predictable and fast, so the user's attention could stay on the product and not on the interface.

## Key decisions

### Visual system from scratch

Without an existing design system, the first decision was to establish the language before designing screens. Palette built from the logo: black as the dominant base, with accents that reinforced the futurist feel without falling into the neon cliché. Typography with contrast between the mechanical and the editorial. Image treatment that assumed photography with dark or neutral backgrounds.

What we dropped: a more accessible and clear system in the conventional sense. Early versions had more contrast and were more "readable." But they lost the brand's personality. The decision was to commit to the identity and work legibility within that world, not outside it.

### Architecture and purchase flows

The work started in FigJam mapping the main flows before touching Figma: product discovery (home and catalog), product detail, cart, and checkout. With a new brand, the discovery flow had to do extra work: not just show products, but build brand context for users arriving with no prior reference.

The hierarchy decision for home: don't start with products. Start with the brand. An editorial entry — large image, short message, no price in view — before the catalog.

What we dropped: a hero with a product carousel. It's the default for almost every fashion ecommerce. It's also what makes them all look the same.

### Product cards and catalog

With a dark aesthetic, cards with white backgrounds break visual coherence. Cards with dark backgrounds lose definition if photography isn't well controlled. We solved this with an adaptive card system: background that inherits the tone of the product image, with a subtle overlay for text legibility.

Catalog filters were designed as a collapsible side panel on desktop and a bottom drawer on mobile. Functional without being the dominant element on the page.

### Checkout and mobile

Checkout was the most conservative section of the project, intentionally. At the point of purchase, familiarity is an advantage. Brand personality appears in details: typography, system messages, confirmation states. The structure is standard and predictable.

Responsive design was a priority from the first wireframe. Desktop screens weren't the adapted reference — they were conceived in parallel with mobile as the primary use context.

## Solution

EEVA Studios now has a digital presence that communicates its identity coherently and enables frictionless purchasing. The visual system establishes its own language that can scale to future collections, new product categories, and editorial content.

The ecommerce covers the complete flow: editorial home, filtered catalog, product detail, cart, checkout, and confirmation. The responsive prototype was delivered ready for handoff to development, with a Figma design system covering components, states, spacing, and visual application rules.

> The brand went from having a logo to having a complete digital experience with its own personality and functional structure.

## Reflection

The project confirmed something that applies to all product design but becomes especially visible in fashion: when aesthetics is part of the value proposition, visual decisions are business decisions. You can't separate "how it looks" from "how well it works for selling."

What I'd do differently: I would have documented the photography rules more explicitly as part of the design system. The visual system assumes a certain type of image — dark, high contrast, controlled background — but that stayed implicit in the result rather than formalized as a guide. For a brand that's going to grow and produce more content, that's a debt that shows up quickly.
